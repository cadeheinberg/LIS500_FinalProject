Group Analysis

We began our process by making our implicit bias survey was to find group members to work on the project with. We formed a group by using canvas discussion tools to contact each other. Once our group was formed, we met over Zoom to talk about our ideas for the project. Although we could not meet in person, we formed meaningful connections with each other and found things in common that we wanted to explore further in our survey.

In our first zoom meeting, we looked at surveys like the Harvard Implicit Bias and began brainstorming different ideas for how we would host the site, the theme of it, and most importantly the questions. For hosting the website, we first investigated WordPress. WordPress offered great services and themes, but we would not be able to add our own code. We faced issues uploading our own files and creating PHP pages. We went with GitHub instead. Although PHP pages aren’t supported, we would have complete control over our code and multiple group members can submit proposals for changes to be made to the site since they could download the source code and make modifications themselves.

Over the next few weeks, we built the site from scratch and incorporated different pages that we thought our users would find engaging. We decided to create a survey about Gender Bias in Tech based on our experiences and we were also inspired by the book Algorithms of Oppression. The focus of the survey was on people’s perspectives about what it is like for different genders to work and be a part of the tech industry. We formatted our questions similarly to each other, and we formatted the questions in a way to be able to measure different levels of bias a surveyor might have. Algorithms of Oppression illustrated the underlying racism and sexism that is exhibited just by using search engines such as Google. Google, being one of the largest tech companies in the world, has created search results with “autosuggestion features including women should not have rights to vote, work, drive, be trusted” and much more (p.15). If these search results were feasible and unnoticed by many, it led us to question the environment of the company that creates this search engine.

By focusing our survey on Gender Bias in tech, we could question the environments of large tech corporations and future tech employees. Technology companies pride themselves on being meritocratic, however after watching the PBS interview “How Ellen Pao realized women ‘cannot succeed’ in Silicon Valley frat boy culture”, it became clear that gender bias is extremely relevant in Silicon Valley, regardless of a meritocratic environment. We wanted to construct a survey that both identifies unconscious biases regarding the current gender gap in tech as well as what policies have led to this environment.

To improve our survey, we enlisted the help of our peers from the course. We received feedback on our design and accessibility, as well as some confusion with the wording of our questions. We modified our code so that visitors were seeing less redundancy in our page overview and so that users were able to identify and put a name to each member of our team. Students pointed out that our survey was embedded improperly and provided us with advice on how we could improve the layout of our test. This resulted in us incorporating Formfacade as a plugin to our Google Form so that we could gather data easily and provide our test takers with their results based on our bias scale. This allowed our users to see where they fall in having or not having a bias concerning gender in the tech industry.

When deciding what kind of questions we would like to ask our survey participants, we collectively agreed to incorporate concepts we learned throughout the course. We decided to include a question about imposter syndrome, which is defined as someone believing they are insufficient in their work and their position, making them feel as though they are an imposter. We learned that this could result in people not receiving critical feedback. To gain insight into whether people believe that this syndrome exists in the tech industry, we decided to ask if people believe that women specifically are more prone to having imposter syndrome in the tech industry. Since most of our test takers were male, we believe that we don’t have enough information to gain a full understanding of how someone may be biased against women feeling as though they are an imposter in a predominantly male industry. Additionally, although we have included references so that test takers can gain a better understanding of the term, we are left not knowing how everyone conceptualizes the term.

The results from our survey showed very different results between males and females. Male test takers were the dominant demographic of our results and they scored around the high 30s and female test takers scored around the low 20s indicating that there is a higher level of bias among men towards women who work in the tech industry. Male test-takers agreed with statements such as “Women in executive positions are more likely to have imposter syndrome in the tech industry in comparison to their male counterparts” and “I believe that women are less likely to receive mentoring and career development opportunities in the tech industry compared to men”. Additionally, from many articles included in our course material, it is clear many people are calling for a change in the tech industry; however, our survey results portray that 34% of users feel “Neutral” about whether a change is necessary. To those who do see a need for a change in the tech industry, what specific steps do you think will be most meaningful toward closing the gender gap? For those who do not see the need for change, do you feel like an environment currently exists that values female voices in the industry?
