Marley's Essay

Before completing this course, I had limited to no experience working with a variety of platforms focused on including HTML, PHP, CSS, etc. Instead of taking a simple introduction to programming course, I chose LIS 500 in hopes of not only developing my programming skills but additionally becoming more aware of the unconscious biases and stereotypes that occur through technology. While this course was most definitely challenging, it was extremely rewarding, specifically my takeaways from this project.

As we began developing our survey, I initially thought about "Algorithms of Oppression”, in which we learned that search results vary depending on the user that is searching them. That is a primary example of intersectionality in tech, and we wanted to ensure that our survey was uniform for all survey takers, no matter their prior internet usage and demographic characteristics. By doing this we ensured that our survey was anonymous and had the same questions for all users. Additionally, earlier in the semester I specifically took the Harvard Implicit Bias test focused on Gender Career. While taking it, I noticed a lack of questions regarding those with actual and first-hand experiences of biases in the industry. We wanted our project to specifically address issues learned in the course such as “imposter syndrome” and “meritocracy” that specifically provide results on unconscious biases for individuals' experiences in the workplace. By doing so, we included a primary question that identifies a pattern of women feeling imposter syndrome in the tech workplace more than men. Beth Andres Beck’s article “Confidence Through Feedback, or Why Imposter Syndrome is the Wrong Metaphor", states the deeper reasoning as to why imposter syndrome occurs and what it means, “Imposter syndrome is often presented as a personal failing. A lack of confidence, our wrong-headed beliefs not matching the reality of how competent we are, or worse of a flaw of our gender”. This completely shifted my thinking to realize that women experience increased imposter syndrome because they are in environments where they don’t have sufficient role models and therefore feel less secure and confident in their positions. Including a survey question about both female tech role models and imposter syndrome works to shift survey takers' mindsets to understand that unconscious biases can be relevant to these less obvious topics and involve real-life experiences for women in the industry.

When considering the remote-paired programming environment that my group members and I collaborated within when creating our final project, it was interesting to see the differences in which all of us tackled the assignment. We each had a variety of opinions on the topic and aesthetics of the website that we needed to work out via Zoom. All of us had experienced the course in different ways and had different backgrounds which resulted in various levels of comfortability towards programming for the project. Some of us felt more comfortable sharing our screens and teaching each other, while others did not. We eventually developed a remote environment where we all felt safe to share our thoughts and experiences, ask each other questions, and insert our opinions. I initially thought the paired programming would consist of one person coding while the others watch, after practice and an understanding of each other's strengths we were able to entrust each other with different aspects of the projects, as well as learn skills from each teammate regarding edits to the code and design of the website.

When we began to design our site, I individually reflected on the Video Lecture Material “Digital Aesthetics & Race” and a course reading regarding aesthetics “Digital Gates”, which we read at the beginning of the semester. The look and aesthetics of our website result in potential changes of use and stereotypical results. For example, different audiences utilized MySpace from Facebook, “MySpace carefully cultivated a demeanor that is personal, whimsical and at times, oppositional. Facebook, on the other hand, has maintained a relatively stable and uniform presentation” (Page 81, Digital Gates). Using the knowledge we gained from this source, we wanted to develop a website that was more Scandinavian style. Simple yet still colorful and enjoyable for all users. We felt that it was important to all explain our preferences and after that delegate different aspects to different peers. That way we ensured that we were aware of all of our opinions when developing a website that could address intersectionality issues.

Overall, this course and the final project allowed me to take a deeper look into all material that I both create for the web as well as the content I interact with online. I have learned the prime aspects of intersectionality, racial and gender disparities in the technology industries, the details of meritocracy, and much more. This project allowed me to not only further my skills in programming but also learn how to interact with peers when programming to develop a final product.
